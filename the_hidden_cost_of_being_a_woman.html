The law is finally EU-wide. That doesn't mean it's enforced EU-wide yet.

Women in the EU earn 11.1% less per hour than men, unadjusted, ranging from -0.8% in Luxembourg to 18.8% in Estonia. This has barely moved in a decade.

The Pay Transparency Directive (EU) 2023/970 was supposed to change that. It gives job applicants the right to know a salary range before interview, bans employers from asking your salary history, and gives every worker the right to request average pay by gender for equivalent work. Member states had until 7 June 2026, last month, to write this into national law. Where they haven't, the clearest parts of the Directive still apply directly, under long-standing EU court precedent.

But the reporting duty that actually forces companies to check their own numbers is staggered by size. Employers with 250 or more staff report from 2027. Companies with 150 to 249 staff report every three years, also from 2027. Companies with 100 to 149 staff don't have to report until 2031. Under 100 staff, never mandatory. Most working women in Europe are employed somewhere in that gap.

This calculator counts both. The pay gap the Directive is designed to close. And the years-long reporting gap that means most employers won't be checked for a long time yet.
